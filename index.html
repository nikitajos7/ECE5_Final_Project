Parts: Arduino Mega 2560, chassis given by class, 2 motors, 2 wheels , jumper wires, breadboards, 7 photoresistors, 7 resistors, 4 potentiometers, motot shield, cardboard (for makeshift light shield and shelf), and lots of tape.
Assembly: While building our prototype one of the biggest issues was finding a place for all of the different components. This was especially hard since the chassis we designed wasn't ready yet, so we had to make the part we added to the chassis with tape. Another issue we came into was the jumper wires, as they went all of the place and sometimes dragged on the floor. The jumper wires to connect the photoresistors were the hardest to deal with because the breadboard for the photoresistors has to face down, so the wire would just stick out. We were able to fix this in the final robot by soldering the wires, so the wires could connect from the top and the photoresistors from the bottom.

PID Control

Our default PID values were:
S = 8
P = 30
I = 0
D = 18
We chose these values because it allowed for the right amount of speed, our nominal speed was very high, while also allowing the robot to turn a little to ensure that it can notice changes in the track.
These values changed with each track depending on how many twists and turns there were and how important speed was for the ranking. For example, our speed for the frequency sweep was set to alomst 0 if not 0 because speed wasn't required for this track. We also increased P and D for this track because of the sharp turns it had to complete. This was also the case for the circuit loop since it also had sharp turns as well. On the other hand, for the drag race there were barely any twists and turns so our P and D values could be very close to 0 and our speed was able to be maxed out to allow the robot to go faster.

Improvements

Some improvements we could have made was having a better chassis that had more support for the shelf. A few days before the final competition the shelf part of our chassis snapped and even though we we able to put it back together with tape it still interfered with the wheels, causing the wheel to fall off at times. Another improvement that could have been made is have a better error calculating algorithm. Our robot was able to do well in the drag race, but when it came to making sharp turns the robot failed to calculate how much it should turn to continue to follow the line. This severely affected our performance in the frequency sweep and the circuit loop.
